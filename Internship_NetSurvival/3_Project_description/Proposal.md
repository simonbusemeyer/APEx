# On the impact of the proportion of excess cancer deaths on estimations in net survival analysis

## APEx project’s goals and objectives

Net survival is defined as the survival that would be observed in a hypothetical world where the only possible cause of death would be the studied cancer. Some authors have stated that if fewer than 30% of deaths are due to the studied cancer, models may show convergence issues and large confidence intervals (Perme, Henderson, and Stare 2009). This can occur in older patients with cancers that have a good prognosis (e.g. prostate cancer), since in this case patients will die from other causes because they are very old, and relatively few will die from their cancer. However, to the best of our knowledge, researchers have not yet established the minimum proportion of cancer deaths, either theoretically or empirically. This is partly because, in the context of relative survival, the cause of death is not known, which makes it difficult to set a threshold. The main objective of this project is to conduct a simulation study to approximate this minimum proportion. The secondary objectives of this study include an analysis of the impact of variations in these proportions on net survival estimates and log-rank test statistics. 

Perme, Maja Pohar, Robin Henderson, and Janez Stare. 2009. “An Approach to Estimation in Relative Survival Regression.” *Biostatistics* 10 (1): 136–46. doi:10.1093/biostatistics/kxn021
